Tuesday

  1. Demo: Boston method of overhead wrist alignment

    • Athletes should be in Malasana Pose while observing the demo
    • General note: for some technical issues in weightlifting, you will find coaches at opposite spectrums. As a consumer of weightlifting media, it's important to realize that often people will religiously advocate a particular method for a few potential reasons:
      • They weren't actually taught a particular method, so only teach what came instinctively and worked for them.
      • They learned and coach only one method, so aren't educated and have never tried or coached alternatives. The majority of coaches who insist their method is the one true method won't admit they are uneducated about something. This includes many "famous" coaches.
      • You should try to identify both the strenths and weaknesses of conflicting methods.
      • What's more important is that you eventually pick a particular method and stick with it. Consistent practice with a particular method is usually a better investment than frequently changing your basic technique.
    • If not otherwise coached, most people will hyper flex their wrists in the overhead position.
    • The Boston method is healthier on your wrists, but more difficult to learn and stabilize than the hyperflexion method. The hyperflexion method puts more stress on your connective tissues and requires you to contend with additional forces that can cause you to lose a lift behind you.

  2. Snatch Push Press Behind Neck + Snatch Balance + Snatch w/3-5 secs pause in catch 60x1 65x1 70x1 75x1 80x1

  3. Demo: Minimum bar height to receive clean

    • The higher you lift the bar (regardless of the lift), the more power is required to successfully complete the same weight.
    • A good strategy is to learn to be able to get under the bar at the lowest height possible. Otherwise the only way to lift more weight is to keep getting stronger. A technically efficient lifter strives to use the first strategy as much as the second.
    • Most people think the bar needs to be lifted higher than is actually necessary than is actually required to get under a clean or snatch.
    • One reason is due to fear: you instinctively want to give yourself an extra margin of error to get under.
    • A second reason is inadequate body awareness; you may underestimate how high the bar is relative to your actual height.
    • A third reason is incorrect timing. You may rebend your legs late, so the bar starts to decelerate before you can actually get under.
